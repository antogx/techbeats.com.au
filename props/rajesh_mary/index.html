Property Indicators Explained29▾
Demand to Supply Ratio
Recommended: more than 46. DSR is a demand-versus-supply score out of 100. A higher score generally means demand is stronger compared with available supply, which may support capital growth potential. DSR Data says the DSR looks at multiple indicators including days on market, vendor discount, auction clearance rate, renter proportion, vacancy rate, yield, stock on market and online search interest.
Example: a suburb with strong buyer demand, low vacancy and low stock for sale will usually score better than a suburb with weak demand and many listings.
Search Interest
Recommended: more than 5. Search Interest measures online buyer demand compared with the number of properties available for sale. DSR Data explains Online Search Interest as the number of people searching online for property divided by the number of properties available for sale.
Example: if 50 people are searching and there are 10 properties for sale, the search interest score is 5.
Higher search interest may indicate stronger demand.
Renters %
Recommended: 5% to 51%. This shows the percentage of people renting in the market. It helps indicate the balance between owner-occupiers and renters. A very high renter percentage may mean more rental competition, while a very low renter percentage may mean a smaller rental market.
Example: if a suburb has 1,000 residents and 400 are tenants, the renter percentage is 40%.
Stock %
Recommended: less than 3.5%. This shows the percentage of properties in the suburb currently listed for sale. Lower stock generally indicates limited supply, which can support capital growth when demand is strong.
Example: if a suburb has 1,000 properties and 10 are listed for sale, Stock % is 1%.
Statistical Reliability
Recommended: more than 30, but 50+ is stronger. This indicates how reliable the market data is. Higher reliability means there is more confidence in the numbers. A score around 30 should be treated as usable but with caution; DSR Data says 50+ is better when relying on DSR.
Example: a small suburb with very few sales may have a high DSR but low reliability, so manual checking is important.
Vacancy
Recommended: less than 3%. Vacancy shows the percentage of rental properties currently vacant. Lower vacancy generally supports stronger rental demand, better rent security and lower vacancy loss.
Example: if there are 200 rental properties and 4 are vacant, vacancy is 2%.
Beds
Number of bedrooms. The right bedroom count as per local demographic can improve rental demand, resale demand and long-term appeal.
Suburb Category
Suburb/location category used as a general lending and risk guide. Most banks may lend across all suburb categories, but some non-bank lenders may have restrictions for Category 4 Suburbs, especially in smaller regional or higher-risk locations. Buyers should confirm lending suitability with their broker or lender.
Bush Fire
Indicates whether the property may be in or near a bushfire-prone area. Sometimes this may be triggered by nearby vegetation, bushland or green zones, so it should be cross-checked with insurance quotes and official maps. It may affect insurance cost, building requirements, buyer demand and resale risk.
Flood
Indicates possible flood exposure. Higher flood risk can increase insurance cost, reduce buyer demand and may affect lending or resale. It can be confirmed with council flood mapping, official flood reports and insurance providers.
Heritage
Indicates whether heritage rules may apply. Heritage is not always negative; it can preserve character and reduce future supply in an area, which may support scarcity. However, it may restrict renovation, demolition or development options.
LGA
Local Government Area or council. This matters because council rules, rates, infrastructure, zoning, planning controls and growth policies can differ between LGAs.
LGA Population
Population size of the wider council area. A larger or growing population may support services, employment, infrastructure, buyer demand and rental demand.
Insurance
Estimated yearly insurance cost. Higher insurance can reduce cash flow and may also indicate higher property risk, such as flood, bushfire, storm, cyclone or building-related risk.
Land
Land size of the property. Larger land may provide scarcity, future extension or development potential, subject to council approval. In general, land is the component that tends to appreciate over time, while the building usually depreciates and requires maintenance.
Built
Approximate year built or age of the property. Age helps estimate likely maintenance, depreciation and renovation needs. As a general preference, properties within roughly the last 50 years may be easier to manage, but condition is more important than age alone. Very old properties can still be considered if it is recently renovated.
Price
Asking price or expected purchase price.
CBA Valuation
Bank or automated valuation estimate. If the estimate is higher than the asking price, it may suggest the property is priced below estimated market value. If it is lower, extra caution and further comparable sales checking may be required.
Corelogic Valuation
RP Data/CoreLogic-style automated price estimate. This is useful as a guide, but it is not a formal valuation. It should be compared with recent comparable sales, agent feedback and other valuation sources.
Domain Valuation
Domain estimated property value. This is another reference point to compare against the asking price and other valuation estimates.
PropTrack Valuation
PropTrack automated valuation estimate. This is useful as a guide, but should not be relied on alone. Always compare it with recent comparable sales and local agent feedback.
Rent (RP)
Estimated rent from RP/CoreLogic-style data. This helps estimate likely rental income and gross yield.
Public Housing
Approximate level of public or social housing in the suburb. A higher level does not automatically make a suburb unsuitable, but it should be considered together with demand, crime, employment, schools, tenant profile and resale appeal.
Suburb Population
Population of the suburb. A reasonable population base can mean a deeper buyer and renter market. Very small markets may have fewer comparable sales and lower liquidity.
Occupancy
Shows whether the property is vacant, tenanted or owner-occupied. A tenanted property may provide income from settlement, while a vacant property may give the investor more control to select a new tenant and set market rent.
Rent
Advertised rent from the listing or agent. This is a useful starting point, but should be compared with current market rent and comparable rentals.
Current Rent
Rent currently being paid by the existing tenant. This is important for immediate cash flow. It may be below market if the lease is old.
Market Rent
Estimated achievable rent based on comparable rentals, agent feedback or market evidence. This helps estimate future cash flow after lease renewal or re-letting.
Yield
Gross rental yield, usually calculated as annual rent ÷ purchase price × 100. Higher yield generally improves cash flow, but buyers should also consider growth potential, risk, vacancy and maintenance.
Example: if rent is $500 per week and price is $500,000, gross yield is $500 × 52 ÷ $500,000 = 5.2%.
